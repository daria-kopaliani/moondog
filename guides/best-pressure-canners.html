The best pressure canners & canning gear (2026)

Before the gear: the kind of food you're canning decides the canner you need — and for low-acid food, that's not negotiable. Here are the picks once you know which method is required.

Most canning-gear lists skip the one fact that matters most: a pressure canner and a water-bath canner are not interchangeable. Low-acid foods — all plain vegetables, meats, poultry, seafood, stocks, and un-acidified tomatoes — require pressure canning to reach the ~240°F that destroys botulinum spores. A boiling-water bath tops out at 212°F and is not a substitute. (More on that line below, and in the linked guides.) So buy the canner the food requires, not the one that's cheaper or already in the cupboard. With that settled, here are the picks.

Pressure canners

If you only buy one thing for life, it's the All American 921. It's a 21.5-quart cast-aluminum canner (7 quarts or 19 pints per batch) built by Wisconsin Aluminum Foundry since the 1930s, and its defining feature is the precision-machined metal-to-metal seal — there's no rubber gasket to crack, dry out, or replace, ever. It ships with both a weighted regulator and a dial gauge, so you get redundancy out of the box. It's the expensive option, and it's the one serious canners hand down.

For most households, though, the Presto 23-quart pressure canner does the same safe job at a fraction of the price. It uses a replaceable gasket and a dial gauge — slightly more maintenance, but the standard recommendation for beginners and budget-conscious canners. Important: a dial gauge must be tested for accuracy once a year (most county extension offices do it free or cheap); a gauge reading 2 psi high can quietly under-process every batch. If you'd rather skip annual testing, add a Presto 3-piece weighted regulator, which rocks at a fixed pressure and can't drift out of calibration.

Weighted vs. dial gauge — the short version.

A weighted gauge holds a fixed pressure (5/10/15 psi) by jiggling or rocking and never needs calibration — but it can't fine-tune, so above 1,000 ft it jumps straight to 15 psi. A dial gauge shows the exact pressure and lets you step up gradually with altitude, but it must be tested every year. The All American 921 gives you both.

Water-bath canners (high-acid foods only)

For jams, jellies, pickles, properly acidified salsa, and most fruits, a water-bath canner is all you need and a pressure canner is overkill. Any tall pot deep enough to cover the jars by an inch or two works, but a dedicated water-bath canner with a rack keeps jars off the bottom (so they don't crack) and makes loading and lifting far easier. The classic enamel Granite Ware pot and Presto's canner-with-rack are both inexpensive and last for years. Just remember the boundary: this pot is for high-acid foods only — it cannot safely process anything that needs a pressure canner.

Jars, lids & rings

USDA-tested recipes assume real canning jars, so use Ball or Kerr mason jars in the size your recipe calls for — pint and quart, regular and wide-mouth, cover most of what home canners do. Bands (rings) are reusable, but flat lids are single-use for canning: their sealing compound only seats reliably once, so keep a stock of fresh replacement lids on hand. Don't reuse old commercial jars (pasta sauce, pickles) for canning — they're not made for repeated heat cycles and aren't part of any tested process.

The tool kit (funnel, jar lifter, and friends)

You can improvise, but a cheap 6-piece canning tool kit pays for itself the first day. The two pieces you'll actually fight without are the jar lifter (lifting a 240°F quart jar out of a canner with regular tongs is how people get burned) and the wide-mouth canning funnel (keeps headspace clean so lids seal). The kit also typically includes a bubble remover / headspace tool, a magnetic lid lifter, and a jar wrench — all minor, all genuinely useful. The Norpro and stainless-steel starter sets are the usual recommendations.

Spare parts: gaskets & overpressure plugs

This section only applies to gasketed canners (Presto and most non-All-American models) — the All American's metal-to-metal seal has no gasket to replace. On a Presto, the rubber sealing ring is a wear item: it dries out, hardens, and eventually won't hold pressure, usually every few years with regular use. Keep a spare canner sealing ring (gasket) and a fresh overpressure plug on hand so a perished gasket doesn't cancel a canning day mid-batch. Replace the gasket the moment it stops sealing cleanly, not after.

The safety line, one more time

  • High-acid food (pH 4.6 or below — most fruits, jams, pickles, acidified tomatoes) — a water-bath canner is sufficient.
  • Low-acid food (above pH 4.6 — all vegetables, meats, poultry, seafood, stocks, plain tomatoes) — a pressure canner is required, per USDA / NCHFP. No exceptions, no "my grandmother did it in a water bath."
  • Tomatoes sit near the line and USDA recipes call for added acid (lemon juice or citric acid) for safe water-bath processing.
Buy the canner the food requires.

If you'll ever can vegetables, meat, or broth, you need a pressure canner — a water-bath setup can't make those foods safe at any time or temperature. If you're only doing jams and pickles, save the money and get a water-bath canner. Plenty of people own both.
