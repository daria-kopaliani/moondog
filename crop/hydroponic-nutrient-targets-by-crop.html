Hydroponic nutrient chart for vegetables, by crop and stage

Lettuce isn't tomato — EC and ratios shift by crop and growth stage.

There's no single "hydroponic nutrient strength." Leafy greens want a lean, nitrogen-forward solution; fruiting crops want a richer mix with more potassium as they set fruit. The chart below covers nine common crops by growth stage. Unlike most nutrient charts online, every row here traces to published university controlled-environment research — Cornell CEA, Penn State Extension, and UF/IFAS — not a brand feed schedule.

Targets shift by stage

Within a crop, the solution changes as the plant develops:

  • Seedling / early: run EC at the low end — young roots are sensitive and high salts cause tip burn.
  • Vegetative: nitrogen-forward to drive leaf and stem growth; raise EC toward the crop's normal range.
  • Flowering / fruiting: for fruiting crops, shift toward more potassium (and relatively less nitrogen) to support fruit set and quality, often at the top of the EC range.

Watch EC drift, not just the starting number. If reservoir EC climbs over a few days, plants are drinking water faster than nutrients — dilute with plain water. If it falls, they're taking up salts faster than water. The trend tells you more than any single reading.

FAQ

What EC should lettuce be in hydroponics?
Seedlings want a low 0.8–1.2 mS/cm (400–600 ppm on the 500 scale); a mature head takes 1.4–1.8 mS/cm (700–900 ppm). Run the low end early so the salts don't burn young root tips. (Cornell CEA.)

What is the ppm range for hydroponic tomatoes?
It climbs through the season: about 1.2–1.6 mS/cm as seedlings, 1.8–2.2 vegetative, then 2.2–2.8 at fruit set (roughly 1100–1400 ppm on the 500 scale), shifting toward more potassium as the plant sets fruit. (Penn State / UF/IFAS.)

What's the difference between the 500 and 700 ppm scale?
Same EC reading, two conversions. The 500 scale is EC × 500 (Hanna, Bluelab); the 700 scale is EC × 700 (Truncheon, HM Digital). The same solution shows a bigger number on the 700 scale, so check which your meter uses before chasing a ppm target.

What pH is best for hydroponic nutrients?
About 5.5–6.5 for most vegetables and herbs, where the most nutrients stay available to the roots.
